> I found that I cannot use AI to learn/solve philosophical thinking problems.
> Professionals can easily solve problems that I couldn't resolve through years of thinking/reading.
> Reflecting on myself, perhaps the AI I use is also just searching within "my" limited vector space.
>
> This led me to wonder, what kind of person truly "knows how to use" AI?

Older people use new tools to solve existing problems, such as work issues; while children/students use AI to solve academic problems.

Often, we think that compared to "adult" problems like work, the academic problems faced by younger people are simpler and more systematic. However, in reality, younger people don't have the time to learn the "meta-problem" of "how to learn"—here, "meta-problem" means they don't have time to develop the habit of taking the "traditional old path." Therefore, they have ample motivation to find the "shortest path" they prefer, and this shortest path will inevitably be validated afterward by assessment methods like "exams."

The distinct characteristics of academics itself are:

1.  Short duration of stages.
2.  Verifiability.

We have a concrete score, and the entire system design further maximizes the cycle of these two characteristics.

In contrast, most adults, when facing so-called "adult problems," often "know" and have the ability to quickly search for existing solutions to adult problems. AI, therefore, appears to them more as a "fast tool," a possible shortcut.

Thus, we will see a common phenomenon: adults encounter problems and try AI. If it's not within their own expertise, there are two possibilities:

1.  Unable to use AI correctly to solve the encountered problem. This doesn't necessarily mean being unable to utilize AI, but more often points to giving up halfway.
2.  Obtain an incorrect solution through AI, because most adult problems cannot be scored; they are more a matter of experience.

In summary, we will see a very interesting and widespread explanation for the fundamental reason why younger children are more likely to master new tools better.

Because children have academics as their playground, and children lack ready-made tools and experience, it pushes children/students to master new tools to find the shortest path, meaning they will use what is called the broadest and deepest search.

It's important to emphasize that the variable truly at play here is not "age," but "whether there is still a traditional old path to fall back on." The advantage of students stems from being institutionally deprived of the old path—not because they are young, but because path dependency has not yet formed. Career changers among adults, novice practitioners, and frontier researchers are actually isomorphic to students; and among students, if the old path (rote memorization, copying answers) remains effective, they will also retreat to it. So a more accurate statement is: AI does not favor the young more; it simply favors those who have no way back. Academics happen to be an institutionalized form of "having no way back."

This also incidentally solves another problem: over what time span a new thing will be popularized or disproven.

Basically, it's the stage from when the "last cohort" of students who have fully mastered and can flexibly apply the new thing grow up and join society to actually generate productivity.
