> I found that I cannot use AI to learn or solve philosophical thinking problems.

Older individuals use new tools to solve existing problems, such as work-related issues; whereas children/students use AI to address academic problems.

Often, we believe that compared to "adult" problems like work, the academic challenges faced by younger people are simpler and more structured. However, in reality, younger individuals do not have the time to learn the meta-question of "how to learn," so they are instead highly motivated to find their preferred "shortest path," which will inevitably be validated later by assessment methods such as "exams."

The distinct characteristics of academics itself are:

1. Short duration of stages.
2. Verifiability.

We have a concrete score, and the entire system design maximizes the acceleration of the cycle of these two characteristics.

In contrast, most adults, when facing so-called "adult problems," often "know" and have the ability to quickly search for existing solutions to adult problems. Therefore, AI is seen by them more as a "quick tool," or a possible shortcut.

Thus, we will observe a common phenomenon: adults will try AI when they encounter problems. If it is not within their own field of expertise, there are two possibilities:

1. They are unable to correctly use AI to solve the encountered problem. This does not necessarily mean they cannot utilize AI, but rather points more to giving up halfway.
2. They obtain an incorrect solution through AI, because most adult problems cannot be scored and are more a matter of experience.

In summary, we will see a very interesting and fundamental reason that universally explains why younger individuals are more likely to master new tools better.

Because children have academics as their playground, and they lack ready-made tools and experience, it drives children/students to master new tools to find the shortest path, meaning they will use what is called the broadest and deepest search.

This also incidentally solves another problem: how long it takes for a new thing to be widely adopted or disproven.

Essentially, it is the stage when the "last cohort" of students who have fully mastered and can flexibly apply the new thing grows up and enters society to actually generate productivity.
