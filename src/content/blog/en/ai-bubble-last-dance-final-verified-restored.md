# The Last Dance Under the AI Bubble

> I started buying gold after Silicon Valley Bank collapsed on March 10, 2023. The FDIC later confirmed that SVB was closed by California regulators on March 10, 2023, and announced protection for all depositors on March 13; the FDIC's deposit insurance reform report that same year also placed this event in the context of re-examining the U.S. deposit insurance system. [G1][G2] Since then, gold has tripled.
>
> Gold is about to return to its essence and challenge the authority of the dollar once again.
>
> Today, June 12, 2026, SpaceX goes public. The IPO to test market appetite has arrived.

## Stagflation

The first time I realized something was off was because of a glaring detail in the October 2025 earnings reports of major U.S. banks. After the pandemic, global economic growth was already like a capsized boat in a ditch, barely alive.

That day, several major U.S. banks released their third-quarter earnings. On the surface, the market heard a very pretty story: the U.S. consumer remained "resilient," bank performance remained strong, and card spending continued to grow. Reuters later reported that Wall Street's top banks highlighted consumer resilience in their Q3 earnings, with customer activity remaining solid, including increased credit and debit card spending, and credit trends still very good. [C1] On the same day, bank stocks were also traded as good news: Reuters' headline was "Wall Street ends mixed; banks rally on upbeat results," with Wells Fargo rising on better-than-expected Q3 profits, Citigroup also rising, and the S&P 500 bank index strengthening. [C2]

But what really struck me as odd was that credit cards were not a peripheral item in the earnings; they were directly written into the performance structure of these banks.

JPMorgan's Q3 2025 earnings showed that its debit and credit card sales volume increased 9% year-over-year; Card Services & Auto net revenue reached $7.2 billion, up 12% year-over-year, driven in part by higher Card Services net interest income from increased credit card revolving balances. [C3] Wells Fargo's Q3 materials also showed that card fees increased by $127 million, up 12% year-over-year, due to higher merchant processing card fees and increased consumer credit card activity; its Credit Card business grew 13% year-over-year, including higher loan balances and higher card fees. [C4] Citigroup's Q3 report similarly stated that U.S. Personal Banking net income grew 64% year-over-year, and Branded Cards revenue reached $3.0 billion, up 8% year-over-year, driven by higher loan spreads, higher interest-earning balances, and higher interchange. [C5]

This is where I first sensed that "stagflation" was taking hold.

The "consumer resilience" in bank earnings might not represent spending power in ordinary people's lives, but rather revolving credit capacity.

Ordinary people continuing to swipe their cards does not necessarily signify prosperity. It is more likely that cash flow has been squeezed tighter by living costs, and they are using high-interest credit cards to roll over debt: using this card to maintain daily life, and using the next paycheck, another card, balance transfers, cash advances, or minimum payments to pay off the previous card, continuously rolling today's inflationary and high-interest-rate pressures into the future.

The so-called resilient consumer may just be revolving debt that hasn't blown up yet.

This is not an emotional judgment. Subsequent data began to confirm this direction. The Philadelphia Fed's large bank credit card data later showed that in Q3 2025, credit card balances and purchase volumes at large banks continued to rise year-over-year, with the most pronounced growth in average purchase volume among borrowers with credit scores below 660; at the same time, revolving balances hit a new high, and the average credit card purchase APR remained near 24.5%, close to historical highs. [C6] The New York Fed subsequently showed that by Q4 2025, U.S. household debt rose to $18.8 trillion, and credit card balances rose to a high of about $1.28 trillion; by Q1 2026, credit card balances, though seasonally lower, were still $70 billion higher than a year earlier, and the share of credit card balances flowing into serious delinquency was 7.10%, up from 7.04% a year earlier. [C7][C8]

This is not a simple story of "consumers are strong," but more like "consumers haven't broken yet, but are already sustaining themselves with high-interest debt."

And this is the most insidious part of stagflation. It may not erupt in the form of a financial crisis, nor necessarily cause a sudden collapse in the job market. It is more like a slow drain on cash flow: inflation pushes up food, gasoline, rent, and daily consumption; high interest rates push up credit card and debt rollover costs; wage growth fails to keep pace with price increases. Macro data may not look broken yet, but the household sector is already paying today's prices with future cash flow.

In Q1 2026, U.S. real GDP annualized growth was only 1.6%, but the PCE price index rose at an annualized rate of 4.5%, and core PCE rose 4.4%; by May, CPI rose 4.2% year-over-year, energy rose 23.5%, and gasoline rose 40.5%. [S1][S2] Employment data appeared stable on the surface, with the U.S. unemployment rate holding at 4.3% in May and nonfarm payrolls increasing by 172,000; but average hourly earnings grew 3.4% year-over-year, already lagging the 4.2% CPI, and the number of long-term unemployed reached 2.0 million, an increase of 524,000 from a year earlier. [S2][S3]

Forecasts and survey data are also converging in the same direction. The Philadelphia Fed's Q2 2026 Survey of Professional Forecasters has already incorporated weaker growth, higher inflation, and higher recession probabilities in subsequent quarters into its forecast path. [S4] The University of Michigan's preliminary May 2026 consumer survey showed that consumer sentiment remains near 2022 lows, with current conditions falling about 9%, as consumers continue to be hit by high prices, gasoline prices, and tariff pressures, and 1-year inflation expectations remain at 4.5%; more importantly, those with little or no stock holdings felt the pressure of high prices significantly more. [S5] Reuters, citing the June Beige Book, reported that U.S. economic activity and inflation both rose in recent weeks, with retail visits declining, credit card usage increasing, and demand for necessities stronger, as middle-income households stretched every dollar further; the same report also mentioned that 9 out of 12 Fed districts cited data center construction. [S6] Another Reuters economist survey also showed that, against the backdrop of persistent war-driven inflation, expectations for rate cuts this year have further faded, and the Fed is likely to continue holding rates. [S7]

This is what I mean by stagflationary pressure: growth is not truly expanding at high speed, but living costs are pressing down on ordinary people again; employment has not collapsed across the board, but real purchasing power is being eroded; consumption has not stopped, but an increasing share of it may be sustained by credit cards and debt rollovers.

My original judgment was that capital did not want to directly trigger a financial crisis, but rather wanted to avoid a financial crisis erupting in an uncontrollable manner. It was more like an attempt to manually engineer a controlled stagflation: letting inflation slowly steal ordinary people's purchasing power, letting high interest rates suppress demand and valuations, and letting asset prices reprice over a long cycle. This way, the crisis would not arrive as a one-day crash, but would be broken down into years of costs—gasoline, food, rent, credit card interest, job anxiety, and declining real wages—paid collectively by all ordinary people.

But what I didn't expect was that it would eventually go a step further, more directly.

If it were just stagflation, capital could at least pretend it was a macro cycle; but when AI companies, cloud providers, chipmakers, data center contractors, and giant platforms like SpaceX begin to centrally sell "the future" to the public markets, things become blunt. They are not waiting for the bubble to naturally deflate, but rather, while the bubble still has narrative power, while retail investors still want to believe in the future, and while index funds and pensions must still allocate to growth assets, they are distributing the last round of risk as evenly as possible.

This is what I call "harvesting the entire society."

Not because AI has no future. On the contrary, AI is definitely the future. The problem is that the future itself cannot prove that all of today's valuations are reasonable, nor can it guarantee that those selling the future today will bear the same kind of risk as those who catch the falling knife tomorrow.

Technological revolutions and financial bubbles have never been mutually exclusive. Classic studies on financial instability, manias-panics-crashes, irrational exuberance, and IPO cycles repeatedly illustrate the same point: when financing, narrative, leverage, and exit windows align, genuine technological progress can also be processed by capital markets into a bubble structure. [B1][B2][B3][B4] Railways, electricity, the internet, and new energy have all proven: technologies that truly change the world often first create a bubble that destroys latecomers. Capital's most adept sleight of hand is packaging "the right direction" as "a reasonable price," "the technology will exist" as "the current company will win," and "the industry will grow" as "those buying in today will make money."

AI is the same.

More cruelly, the companies that look most like winners today may not necessarily be the ones that survive after the bubble. Technology cycles never guarantee that first movers live to the end, nor that the companies doing the best now will continue to do the best in the next paradigm. Today's lead may just be a temporary lead created by financing windows, GPU supply, brand credibility, and distribution channels; when cost structures, hardware forms, algorithm paradigms, or regulatory environments change, today's winners could become tomorrow's obsolete assets.

## OpenAI Infrastructure

OpenAI was the first place I felt something was wrong. As an AI giant, it should be the closest to the future, but its actions looked less like a company that has found a profitable endgame and more like a capital node that must bind everyone into the same growth curve.

The Stargate Project announced plans to invest $500 billion over four years to build new AI infrastructure for OpenAI in the U.S.; participants include OpenAI, SoftBank, Oracle, MGX, with technology partners including Oracle and NVIDIA. [O1][O2] Subsequently, OpenAI reached an AI infrastructure agreement with CoreWeave worth up to approximately $11.9 billion and obtained equity in CoreWeave; Reuters also reported that the deal occurred on the eve of CoreWeave's IPO. [O3][O4] Then came Oracle's roughly $300 billion compute procurement agreement, AMD's 6GW GPU deployment, and Broadcom's 10GW of OpenAI-designed AI accelerators. [O5][O8][O9][O10]

These numbers are so large that they are no longer ordinary business expansion, but the financial structure itself.

If OpenAI already had a clear, stable, self-financing path to profitability, these agreements could be understood as capacity expansion. But reality is more complex. Reuters, citing a WSJ report, noted that as OpenAI races toward an IPO, its revenue and user targets have fallen short of expectations, raising external concerns about whether it can support massive data center spending. [O11] HSBC analysts also estimated that OpenAI may need about $207 billion in new funding to meet its data center spending commitments through 2030, and may still be unprofitable by 2030; this is only an analyst estimate, not a company-confirmed fact, but it sufficiently explains why the market is starting to worry about its funding gap. [O12]

This is hardly ordinary commercialization. It is more like binding cloud providers, chipmakers, data center financiers, U.S. policy narratives, and public market investors all to the same future story.

OpenAI may not be the most malicious player. On the contrary, it may be the one at this table who first realized the bubble was inevitable. Sam Altman knew from the beginning that, whether it was OpenAI or not, AI would be pushed into a bubble. Given that, the most rational move is not to exit, but to make oneself the center of the bubble, binding the entire AI industry, the U.S. government, cloud providers, chipmakers, and capital markets.

It thought the U.S. government would bail out the market, and perhaps eventually it will. But politics is not a stabilizer; politics itself can also become a transaction. Especially when Trump once again ties tariffs, industrial policy, capital market sentiment, and personal political narrative together, the market is no longer just a market—it becomes a K-line chart that can be drawn together by policy, public opinion, and liquidity.

This is the sharper point I wanted to make in my original text: when a capitalist-style president regains the ability to directly influence asset prices, many traditional "policy objectives" get reordered by asset prices. Tariffs can be a negotiating tool, regulation can be a political tool, AI can be a national competition narrative, and the stock market itself can become a dashboard to showcase governance capability. Whether the government will bail out the market is no longer just an economic question, but a question of political price.

## NVIDIA Circular Investment

Originally, every player at the table was gambling, betting they wouldn't be the loser. So everyone went all in, until they all realized they were exhausted. Profits have been compressed to the limit, while the AI economy remains in an internal loop. Model companies need compute, cloud providers need contracts, chipmakers need orders, data centers need financing, electricity and land need policy, and capital markets need growth stories. Each node is validating another node's valuation, and each node needs another node to keep believing.

This is the most dangerous part of so-called circular trading.

It may not necessarily be financial fraud, nor necessarily a conspiracy, but it entangles real demand with capital demand, allowing investment, revenue, equity, contracts, and market confidence to feed on each other. NVIDIA invests in OpenAI, OpenAI procures NVIDIA systems; OpenAI signs massive compute contracts with cloud providers, who use those contracts to finance, go public, and expand, in turn supporting OpenAI's compute narrative. NVIDIA's announcement of up to $100 billion investment in OpenAI, and its partnership with OpenAI to deploy 10GW of NVIDIA systems, is one of the most representative nodes in this structure. [O6][O7][O13][O14]

This structure looks like a synergistic ecosystem in an upcycle, but in a downcycle, it becomes a chain of cross-collateralization.

NVIDIA is actually the most unique. Unlike other companies that can cash out and exit through IPOs, secondary market share sales, or equity financing, as the hardest shovel-seller in the entire AI bubble, its cash-out method has always been selling hardware: selling GPUs, systems, networking, and entire data center infrastructure. Its every move is analyzed by the market, because it can no longer claim to be just a bystander; its customers, investment targets, revenue expectations, and the entire AI infrastructure bubble are tied together. It's not that it can't make money, but that it makes so much money that it can only keep selling hardware, praying to become a second IBM—an infrastructure company that survives the bubble—rather than a hardware cyclical stock that gets revalued when the bubble bursts.

## The Hardware Cost-Effectiveness of LLMs

There is also a technical judgment here that cannot be obscured by capital narratives: what truly fails first for the LLM path may not be the technology itself, but hardware cost-effectiveness.

I believe scaling laws still exist. Larger models, more data, longer training, and more complex reasoning can indeed continue to bring capability improvements. But the problem is, if each capability improvement requires larger clusters, higher inference costs, more complex engineering orchestration, and higher electricity and cooling costs, then the bottleneck is no longer "can it be done," but "is anyone willing to pay for this cost long-term."

When a model must run across dozens or more devices, it may indeed be stronger, but it also begins to move away from the economics of ordinary software. What is truly fatal is not that it isn't intelligent, but that it is too expensive, too slow, too heavy, and too dependent on hardware cycles. API price wars will compress profits, inference costs will eat up cash flow, enterprise deployments will lengthen payment collection cycles, and open-source models will continuously lower the boundaries of what can be charged. The technology path may not have failed, but the commercial cost-effectiveness will fail first.

So I say, the fundamental failure of LLMs is not the technology, but the cost-effectiveness mismatch on hardware. When better hardware arrives, we may indeed have stronger models; but by that day, the algorithm paradigm itself may have already changed. History often goes like this: the first-generation technology path proves the direction, and the second-generation hardware and algorithms truly harvest the world. Those who build it first today may not live to the end; those who do it best today may not continue to be the best in the next phase.

Companies like Oracle and CoreWeave are more like middle layers. Oracle can continue to hold up with its balance sheet, cloud contracts, and capital market narrative; neocloud companies like CoreWeave are more fragile. Their value comes from GPUs, data centers, contracts, and financing capabilities, but if the market begins to doubt the payment ability of model companies, or doubts that AI demand growth can cover capital expenditures, then these companies will shift from "core AI infrastructure assets" to "high-leverage cyclical assets."

The biggest problem for such companies is not a lack of assets, but that the assets are too heavy and the credibility too light. GPUs, server rooms, power contracts, and long-term compute contracts all look like moats in an upcycle; but once the market is no longer willing to value GPUs and long-term compute contracts as growth stocks, they will quickly turn from "future infrastructure" into "hardware contractors weighed down by debt, depreciation, and utilization rates." Giants still have cash flow, ecosystems, and policy relationships to tell stories; neoclouds have a narrower story: if model company demand falters, they will be crushed into the road by the hardware cycle like a steamroller, becoming the sunk cost of the next round of infrastructure.

## Executives Rushing to Cash Out

The most glaring thing about CoreWeave is not just its dependence on GPUs, data centers, massive debt, and a few large customer contracts, but the pace at which insiders are cashing out.

According to a Bloomberg report in June 2026, CoreWeave's stock price has more than doubled since its IPO in March 2025; but during the same period, company executives have sold over $2.3 billion in personal holdings. Bloomberg, citing insider transaction data from Washington Service, noted that the main sellers were CoreWeave's three co-founders: Michael Intrator, Brannin McBee, and Brian Venturo. [CW1] Subsequent market reports further pointed out that these transactions were executed through prearranged trading plans; among them, Brian Venturo sold over $1.1 billion after the lock-up period ended, and the three founders still hold about 18% of the company's shares after the sales, with Intrator remaining the largest shareholder at about 10.4%. [CW2]

This does not mean CoreWeave necessarily has problems, nor that these transactions are illegal. Prearranged trading plans are a common arrangement used by executives of U.S. listed companies for share sales; without further confirmation from individual SEC filings, I am not presenting this as a verified 10b5-1 fact. But when a company has just been pushed onto the public market by the AI infrastructure narrative, its stock price has skyrocketed, its capital expenditures are huge, its debt burden is heavy, and its customer concentration is extremely high, the founding team and executives collectively cashing out over $2 billion is itself a very strong signal: primary market players and insiders have already turned the AI infrastructure story into cash, while public market investors are beginning to bear the valuation, financing, and cyclical risks.

CoreWeave's IPO filings also explain why this matters. Its S-1/A shows that the company issued 47,178,660 shares of Class A common stock in the IPO, and selling shareholders offered 1,821,340 shares; the company will not receive any proceeds from the sale of shares by selling shareholders. At the same time, after the IPO, the three co-founders Michael Intrator, Brian Venturo, and Brannin McBee still held approximately 37.0%, 23.2%, and 18.7% of the voting power, respectively, totaling about 79.0%. [CW3] In other words, CoreWeave is not a company that has been fully publicized in the ordinary sense: control remains highly concentrated, but market risk has been made public.

This is the core structure of the entire AI bubble: control is in the hands of insiders, risk is in the hands of the public market.

## IPO

Google / Alphabet cannot stay out of it either. It is, of course, a genuinely profitable company, with search, advertising, cloud, and Gemini. But when Alphabet announced a massive equity capital raise to expand AI infrastructure and compute, its signaling effect was still very strong. [A1][A2] In the same phase, OpenAI also formally submitted a confidential draft S-1, putting the future listing option on the table. [I1] Even the most cash-flow-rich giant and the most core model company are beginning to connect AI infrastructure, the IPO path, and capital markets more directly, indicating that AI is no longer an internal R&D project, but an infrastructure war that needs to be jointly shouldered by capital markets.

The truly dangerous signal is not that one company is raising funds, but that everyone is raising funds almost simultaneously. Every company is afraid of becoming the last one to step up and ask for money, because the last fundraiser is most easily identified by the market as "already unable to hold on." Thus, the rush itself becomes a run: Google first launches an equity raise, OpenAI quickly puts out its confidential S-1, Anthropic also queues up to go public, and SpaceX packages the Musk platform into the public market. Every company says it is financing for the future, but from the market structure perspective, it looks more like everyone sees the limited number of lifeboats and suddenly starts running.

Anthropic is the same. On one hand, it represents the AI safety narrative; on the other, it submits a confidential draft S-1, preparing for an IPO. [H1] This does not prove Anthropic is hypocritical, nor that it is deliberately harvesting. But it does show that discourses like safety, ethics, alignment, pause, and frontier risk have already coexisted with the capital exit cycle. The Anthropic Institute, in discussing recursive self-improvement, proposed the need to establish verifiable mechanisms to slow down or temporarily pause frontier AI development, which may be sincere in terms of technical governance; but when this appears in the same period as an IPO, what the market sees is not just safety, but "the safety narrative is also going public." [H2]

The problem is not that these safety discussions are necessarily fake, but that they can also become the emotional packaging most easily understood by ordinary investors before an IPO: the more you emphasize "this thing is too powerful and must be regulated," the more you reinforce "this thing represents the future"; the more you talk about frontier risk, the more you tell the market "we are at the frontier." This is the most subtle part of the safety narrative: it can be a sincere governance language, and at the same time, it can become fear marketing for the capital market. Whether you believe its motives or not doesn't matter; the market will translate it into valuation language.

SpaceX takes this to another level.

It is no longer just an aerospace company, nor just a Starlink company. SpaceX's S-1 divides its business into three segments: Space, Connectivity, and AI, and incorporates the AI compute, Grok, and X from the xAI acquisition into the AI segment: rockets and Starship are the physical capability to reach orbit, Starlink / Starshield is communications and government security networks, X is the data and distribution layer, Grok / xAI is the AI layer, and SpaceX becomes the capital and engineering parent of the Musk company matrix. [X1]

The segment data in the S-1 is blunt: in 2025, the Space segment had revenue of about $4.086 billion and an operating loss of $657 million; Connectivity / Starlink had revenue of about $11.387 billion, operating profit of $4.423 billion, and Segment Adjusted EBITDA of about $7.168 billion; the AI segment had revenue of about $3.201 billion, but an operating loss of $6.355 billion, and Segment Adjusted EBITDA of -$1.237 billion. [X1] Even more glaring is the capital expenditure: in 2025, AI segment capex was about $12.727 billion, and in Q1 2026, AI segment capex reached another $7.723 billion. [X1]

In other words, the SpaceX IPO is not simply selling rockets and Starlink to the market, but packaging Starlink's cash flow, xAI/Grok's losses, AI compute capital expenditures, X's platform narrative, U.S. defense and communications networks, and Musk's personal credibility all into a super-platform story that can be purchased by the public market.

So 2026 naturally becomes the harvesting year of the last dance.

This last dance most resembles a financing run. In a normal cycle, companies raise funds according to business maturity; in the late stage of a bubble, companies raise funds according to "who hasn't had a chance to sell their story yet." Everyone is afraid of becoming the clown scrambling for money on the eve of the deadline, because that means the market has already started asking: why do you need money now? Why has everyone else already sold the future, and you're still standing at the door? So the rush itself exposes fear, and fear drives more rushing.

The FOMO sentiment in 2025 was clear to everyone. When OpenClaw exploded in popularity, nearly a thousand developers and AI enthusiasts lined up outside Tencent's Shenzhen headquarters for installation. Sina Tech reported that on March 6, 2026, nearly a thousand people lined up outside Tencent's building, and Tencent Cloud engineers completed free cloud installations of OpenClaw for users; because OpenClaw's local deployment environment is complex, door-to-door installation services had already appeared on platforms like Xiaohongshu, with one-time installation fees in Shenzhen, Guangzhou, Hangzhou, Chongqing, and other cities ranging from 300 to 1,000 yuan; platforms like Xianyu also offered tutorials, one-click installations, and remote assistance services, with prices ranging from tens of yuan to nearly 200 yuan. [OC1] 21st Century Business Herald also reported that nearly a thousand people lined up outside Tencent's Shenzhen office, with door-to-door installation pricing on domestic platforms like Xiaohongshu and Xianyu mostly between 300–800 yuan, 500 yuan per session becoming the mainstream price, and remote installation also costing 50–100 yuan, with some online stores already selling over 1,000 units. [OC2]

This is not just a tool trend; it is the industrialization of FOMO. OpenClaw requires calling large model capabilities, can be deployed locally or in the cloud, and needs model APIs, cloud compute, servers, plugins, and configuration costs behind it. Sina Tech mentioned that Tencent Lighthouse Cloud launched a one-click deployment template for OpenClaw at the end of January, and Baidu AI Cloud launched an ultra-simple deployment solution for OpenClaw in February. [OC1] Cailian Press further reported that domestic cloud providers such as Tencent Cloud, Alibaba Cloud, China Mobile Cloud, China Telecom Cloud, JD Cloud, Volcano Engine, and Baidu AI Cloud all connected to OpenClaw; through one-click deployment, permission encapsulation, security hardening, and other methods, cloud providers transformed the open-source project into a sellable SaaS service, essentially using compute, storage, networking, and engineering capabilities to lower user barriers and seek a closed loop for commercial monetization. [OC3]

At the same time, cloud providers and model companies also launched various Coding Plans. A Tencent Cloud developer article stated that agent workflows like OpenClaw consume tokens at high frequency, with a moderately complex task potentially triggering dozens of model calls; between late 2025 and March 2026, major domestic cloud providers and model companies intensively launched Coding Plan subscription packages that replaced per-token billing with a fixed monthly fee, including Alibaba Cloud Bailian, Volcano Ark, Tencent Cloud, Zhipu, Kimi, MiniMax, and others. [OC4] Sohu tech articles also summarized this phenomenon as the OpenClaw "national shrimp farming" era, with various cloud and model providers launching Coding Plans, exchanging a fixed monthly fee for model call quotas, attempting to solve the token cost anxiety of heavy OpenClaw users. [OC5]

But I believe this is exactly the most typical part of a bubble: at the time, they looked like gateways to a new era, like AI workflows everyone must have; but once the frenzy recedes, many things will become negative assets. Subscriptions, quotas, compute, plugins, workflows, training costs, and team migration costs will all become sunk costs after the bubble recedes. BBC Chinese later reported that a reversal from "raising lobsters" to "unloading lobsters" was emerging: some users felt OpenClaw had high technical barriers, high costs, and little practical use, and began paying for uninstallation; experts also warned that OpenClaw is currently "not very practical," with costs and risks disproportionate. [OC6] People's Daily Online Shenzhen also cautioned that while OpenClaw represents a new trend in AI agents, the deployment barriers, long-term token costs, hardware costs, operation and maintenance costs, and security risks are not low, and ordinary users should not blindly follow the trend. [OC7]

So this is not as simple as "a tool became popular." It is more like a preview of the AI bubble at the ordinary person level: first create anxiety with a new gateway, then lower the barrier with free installation, then lock users into the ecosystem with cloud deployment, Coding Plans, plugin marketplaces, and workflow migration. While the bubble is still here, these costs are interpreted as "learning the future"; after the bubble recedes, they will become bills.

The stock market is the same. As prices chased higher, everyone was tempted. Fund managers couldn't miss AI, pensions couldn't miss AI, indices couldn't be without AI, and retail investors couldn't watch others make money. So everyone knew it was expensive, but everyone was afraid of getting off too early. The strongest moment of a bubble is not when no one knows it's a bubble, but when everyone thinks they can leave before the music stops.

This year, all model companies understood that they need to sell models, to directly sell this concept, to sell the credibility of Gemini, Claude, ChatGPT, Grok in their hands, and exchange it for money, to survive the tough times ahead.

This statement sounds harsh, but it may be the essence of capital markets. The models themselves may not be immediately profitable; API price wars will compress profits, open-source models will lower moats, enterprise deployments will lengthen sales cycles, and inference costs will continuously drain cash. But the fact that "models represent the future" can itself be securitized. As long as the market is still willing to pay for the future, model companies can sell the future to the market.

OpenAI sells the credibility of ChatGPT, Anthropic sells the credibility of Claude and safety, Google sells the credibility of Gemini, and SpaceX / xAI sells the composite credibility of the Musk platform, Grok, and X.

And what ordinary people buy is a future that has already been priced by others.

## Index Funds

The SpaceX IPO is even more critical because of the index mechanism.

Reuters reported that SpaceX chose to list on Nasdaq, planning to use the ticker SPCX, targeting about $75 billion in fundraising and a valuation of about $1.75 trillion, which would make it the largest IPO in history if completed; the same report also mentioned that one of the backgrounds for SpaceX choosing Nasdaq was Nasdaq's introduction of a new fast entry rule, allowing newly listed large-cap companies to enter the Nasdaq-100 more quickly. [X2] Nasdaq's official FAQ spells out the mechanism more clearly: starting May 1, 2026, eligible new Nasdaq-listed companies that rank in the top 40 can be included in the Nasdaq-100 15 trading days after listing, subject to a 3x float cap constraint; Nasdaq also adjusted the market capitalization ranking methodology so that both listed shares and unlisted shares can be used for eligibility / ranking market capitalization, but unlisted shares are used for eligibility and ranking, not for actual weight calculation. [X3]

This means SpaceX is not simply "slowly selected by the market after listing" in the ordinary sense. It has embedded the buying mechanism of index-tracking funds from the design stage of the listing.

Active investors can at least choose whether to buy or not; but ETFs, mutual funds, derivatives, and benchmark strategies tracking the Nasdaq-100, once the rules include SpaceX, must rebalance by weight. Bloomberg described this risk as a "feedback loop," citing Intropic estimates that passive investors could hold about 30% of the free float after fast entry; this is not a confirmed fact that has already occurred, but a potential mechanical buying pressure created by the combination of index rules, low float, and a mega-cap narrative. [X4]

This is what I mean by "the entire society bearing it together."

Ordinary people may not have actively chosen xAI, may not have studied Grok's losses, and may not understand Starship's capital expenditures; but as long as they hold Nasdaq-100, tech index funds, pensions, passive products, or indirectly allocate to U.S. growth assets through asset management accounts, they may become passive bagholders of this IPO through the index mechanism.

This is not a story of everyone actively placing bets in a free market, but a story of financial infrastructure distributing risk to everyone.

Banks first earn income from credit card revolving borrowing, investment banks then earn fees from IPOs and refinancings, index companies set inclusion rules, ETFs and pensions execute rebalancing, and ordinary people finally enter the table through wages, savings, retirement funds, fund accounts, and passive index products. Each layer says it is just a rule executor, but when the rules are strung together, it becomes an extremely efficient risk transfer.

The role of banks and investment banks is especially important here. They don't need to genuinely believe these assets can win long-term, nor do they need to bear the last baton like ordinary investors. They are more like sellers: designing transactions, underwriting issuances, arranging liquidity, writing research reports, organizing roadshows, collecting fees, and then handing the final risk to the public market and passive funds. Saying "acting as a tiger's accomplice" does not mean every banker is subjectively evil, but rather that in this structure, banks naturally stand in the position of sellers and the house. They sell tickets to the future, earn ticket handling fees, and those who truly stay in the venue waiting for the music to stop are the ticket buyers.
