> This refers to Capital Dynamics, which manages global funds like i Capital Global Fund—their performance? Just look at the net asset value trend. Its IT department is chaotic in management; everyone is capable, but there is no real leader, and no one dares to be a leader, after all, this is a department personally deployed and commanded by the CEO.

The CEO personally overseeing the IT department—sounds impressive, right? I thought so too when I first arrived. But the essence quickly revealed itself: **a complete failure of management**.

<!--more-->

## Why?

Because this is a classic case of "the emperor personally leading the campaign" style of management. CEO Tan Teng Boo, the soul of Capital Dynamics, with 30 years of independent investment management and offices in Hong Kong, Sydney, Singapore, and Kuala Lumpur. The company relies almost entirely on his personal charisma and decision-making ability. He knows he is getting old and needs to cultivate a successor—but this is a paradox: without him, the company cannot continue to secure resources; with him, he cannot truly let go.

The CEO personally deploying and commanding the IT department sounds like a focus on technology. Everyone is capable, but there is no real leader—because the leader can only be the CEO himself. Who dares to overstep? The result is a **power vacuum**.

## So

No one provides project completion expectations. When the CEO comes to check, what can everyone do?—"You're anxious, I'm anxious too." Everyone acts anxious, but no one actually pushes things forward. Because pushing forward means responsibility, and responsibility means bearing consequences.

Thus, the company falls into an absurd situation: everyone is talking, but no one is really doing. Everyone learns to talk endlessly in meetings and disappear in action.

Shift blame to others, keep credit for yourself—this is the only survival rule in this system.
